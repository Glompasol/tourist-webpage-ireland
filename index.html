Did You Know?

  • No Snakes - The legend says St. Patrick drove all the snakes out of Ireland, in reality snakes never lived there and it was actually symbolism for driving out paganism with Christianity.
  • Leprechauns are Protected - In Carlingford, Leprechauns are officially recognized as a protected species.
  • A Land of Castles - Ireland has over 30 000 castles. You could visit a new one every day for years.
  • Oldest Pub - The Brazen head in Dublin claims to date back to 1198. That's older than most countries.
  • The Harp Symbol - The national symbol of Ireland isn't the shamrock, its the harp. Even Guinness uses it, flipped upside down, to avoid copyright.
  • First Country to Ban Smoking in Pubs - In 2004, Ireland became the first country in the world to ban smoking in all indoor workplaces including pubs.
  • Guinness World Records Origin - The book was created in the 1950s by the managing director of Guinness Brewery to settle bar arguments. So Guinness isn't just a beer, but a trivia too.
  • Halloween Started Here - The holiday is based on the Celtic festival of Samhain, marking the end of harvest and the start of winter.

Irish Food Menu

Irish Stew

The national comfort dish. Traditionally made with mutton or lamb along with onion, potato, and carrots.

Soda Bread

This bread is baked with baking soda instead of yeast. Every Irish family has their own recipes, often including raisins or seeds. Best served with butter.

Boxty

Classic potato dish from the North and West. Crispy on the outside, soft inside. Made from grated raw potato, mashed potato, flour, and buttermilk.

Seafood Chowder

Creamy soup filled with fresh seafood usually Salmon, cod, and shellfish served with soda bread.

Cuddle

The recipe originated from Irish families throwing together leftovers like sausages, bacon, onion, and sliced potatoes.

Full Irish Breakfast

The monster of breakfasts. Includes sausages, bacon, eggs, black pudding, white pudding, tomatoes, beans, and toast. Designed to fuel farmers through long workdays.

Jameson

Irish Whiskey. Lighter and less smoky than Scotch.

Guinness Beer

The most iconic Irish beverage, brewed in Dublin since 1759. Famous for its creamy head and dark rich flavour. Tourists flock to Guinness storehouse to try it straight from the source.

The History of Ireland

Ireland's story is one of resilience and endurance, and even in tough times the Irish have always found a way to celebrate life whether its through music, dance, or a good pint of Guinness.

Before Christianity, the Celtic people lived in Ireland. Consisting of many different tribes, the Celtic people were known for being warriors, like the Vikings who later invaded Ireland in the 8th century and established settlements. These viking camps would go on to become Ireland's largest coastal cities including Dublin, its capital.

In 1155 the normans invaded Ireland marking the start of a 800 year period of British rule. Ireland under British rule often found itself involved in conflicts such as the wars of the three kingdoms which resulted in more than 220 000 Irish deaths. Furthermore Ireland had numerous famines but the most devastating was the great famine of 1845 which killed more than one million people and forced another million to emigrate taking away 1/4 of the population.

In the 20th century the Irish would see a surge of nationalism and a desire for independence with the Easter rising of 1916. Although the rising was quickly crushed this would inspire many to join the fight and initiated the Irish war of independence from 1919-1921 and would force the british to create a treaty giving Ireland dominion status. However this would lead to the Irish civil war between those who supported the treaty and those who opposed it.

In the 1960's tensions between the Protestant majority and the Catholic minority would rise after a campaign by the Northern Ireland Civil Rights Association to end discrimination against the Catholic minority by the Protestants. The government would attempt to suppress these protests with the police The Royal Ulster Constabulary (RUC), that were overwhelmingly Protestant and known for police brutality particularly against Catholics. Increasing tensions led to the deployment of British troops in Northern Ireland in what would become the British army's longest operation. The British were initially welcomed by some catholics being seen as a peacekeeping force but that trust would quickly dissolve after the Bloody Sunday Massacre in 1972. The main participants in the Troubles were the Provisional Irish Republican Army (IRA) and the Irish National Liberation Army (INLA). The Provisional Irish Republican Army (Provisional IRA) was the most active paramilitary group throughout the Troubles. The Provisional IRA sought to end British Rule in Northern Ireland and facilitate Irish reunification and proclaimed themselves as the sole legitimate successor to the original IRA from the Irish War Of Independence.
